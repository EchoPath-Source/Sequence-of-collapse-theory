# P4 Draft Outline v0.1 — Observer-Dependent Decoherence / CGDS / SOC-MZI

## Working title

**Observer-Dependent Decoherence: Conscious-Access Conditions in Double-Slit and Mach-Zehnder Experiments**

## Abstract placeholder

This paper develops a physics-facing experimental program for the Sequence of Collapse Theory claim that detector interaction and conscious access may represent distinct stages in the measurement process. The anchor protocol is the Consciousness-Gated Double-Slit (CGDS) experiment, which compares machine-only measurement, conscious-access measurement, inaccessible-record measurement, and deleted-record conditions. The paper also proposes a Mach-Zehnder interferometer extension (SOC-MZI) to enable tighter control over path recombination, phase manipulation, and which-path erasure. The goal is to define falsifiable, blinded, preregistered tests of whether conscious access to measurement information modulates interference visibility beyond ordinary detector-induced decoherence.

## 1. Introduction

- State the measurement problem in cautious, physics-facing language.
- Clarify that standard decoherence explains loss of interference through environmental entanglement but does not by itself solve subjective outcome selection.
- Introduce the SoCT distinction between first collapse and second collapse.
- Define conscious access as an experimental variable, not as assumed proof of mind-caused collapse.
- State that the paper's immediate goal is protocol design and falsification.

## 2. Background

### 2.1 Standard double-slit and which-path measurement

- Interference when which-path information is unavailable.
- Interference loss when which-path information is physically marked.
- Detector interaction and environmental decoherence.

### 2.2 Delayed-choice and quantum-eraser experiments

- Information availability and erasure.
- Avoid overclaiming retrocausality.
- Reframe as information-access timing and record accessibility.

### 2.3 Conscious-access question

The source corpus proposes a missing variation: path data is measured but never observed by a human. The test asks whether recorded-but-inaccessible data differs from consciously reviewed data.

## 3. Sequence of Collapse framing

### 3.1 First collapse

Photon, detector, or environment interaction begins localization and decoherence.

### 3.2 Second collapse

Conscious access finalizes the experienced outcome in the SoCT model.

### 3.3 Physics-facing translation

For experimental purposes, this becomes:

> Does the condition of conscious access to measurement records correlate with measurable differences in interference visibility after controlling for detector interaction and environmental decoherence?

## 4. CGDS anchor protocol

### 4.1 Experimental conditions

1. **No-path control**: no which-path marking.
2. **Machine-only inaccessible record**: which-path data recorded but not accessed during the experimental window.
3. **Conscious-access record**: which-path data recorded and reviewed by a human observer in real time or during a defined access window.
4. **Deleted-record condition**: which-path data recorded then destroyed before any conscious access.
5. **Sham-access control**: observers believe they are viewing path data, but they are viewing unrelated or delayed data.

### 4.2 Primary outcome measures

- Fringe visibility.
- Fringe contrast.
- Fourier-domain interference structure.
- Event-rate stability.
- Detector noise and drift.

### 4.3 Secondary measures

- Observer attention state.
- Time of access.
- Subjective focus ratings.
- Blinding integrity.

## 5. SOC-MZI extension

### 5.1 Motivation

Mach-Zehnder interferometers provide controlled beam-splitter paths and phase manipulation, allowing stronger isolation of which-path, recombination, and erasure variables.

### 5.2 Proposed SOC-MZI conditions

- Path indistinguishable.
- Path marked but inaccessible.
- Path marked and consciously accessed.
- Path marked and erased before access.
- Delayed access after detector logging.

### 5.3 Advantages over basic double slit

- Tighter phase control.
- Easier switching between path availability states.
- Cleaner analysis of visibility and path distinguishability.

## 6. Analysis plan

### 6.1 Randomization

- Randomize condition order.
- Blind experimenter where possible.
- Predefine access windows.
- Predefine exclusion criteria.

### 6.2 Statistical tests

- Compare visibility across conditions.
- Test condition-by-time interactions.
- Correct for multiple comparisons.
- Use permutation or bootstrap methods for robustness.

### 6.3 Null model

Standard quantum mechanics predicts that detector interaction and path distinguishability, not conscious review of records, determine interference loss.

### 6.4 SoCT prediction

SoCT predicts a possible difference between inaccessible-record and conscious-access conditions if conscious access acts as a finalization boundary.

## 7. Falsification criteria

P4 is weakened if:

- conscious-access and inaccessible-record conditions show no reliable difference;
- any apparent differences are explained by detector drift, experimenter bias, unblinding, thermal effects, noise, or record-handling artifacts;
- sham-access and real-access conditions behave identically;
- effects fail replication under preregistered conditions.

## 8. Limitations

- Consciousness is difficult to operationalize.
- Detector interaction may fully determine decoherence under standard physics.
- Human-observer protocols introduce bias risks.
- Extraordinary claims require careful controls, replication, and conservative interpretation.

## 9. Discussion

- How a null result sharpens SoCT.
- How a weak positive result would require independent replication.
- Why P4 should remain separate from P1 gravity and P2/P3 cosmology.
- How future quantum computing and brain-field tests should only proceed after CGDS/SOC-MZI protocol discipline is established.

## 10. Conclusion

P4 proposes a preregistered, falsifiable experimental program testing whether conscious access to quantum measurement records has measurable consequences beyond standard detector-induced decoherence. The immediate contribution is a clean protocol architecture, not a claim of confirmed observer-caused collapse.
